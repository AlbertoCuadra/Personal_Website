Introduction

Consider a fresh homogeneous mixture with an uniform flow whose Mach number satisfy the Chapman-Jouguet condition \(\mathcal{M}_u>\mathcal{M}_{\rm cj}=\left(1+Q\right)^{1/2}+Q^{1/2}\), where \(Q\) is the dimensionless heat release per unit mass. We can easily obtain the Rankine-Hugoniot jump conditions from the conservation equations and assuming a calorically perfect gas, namely \[\begin{equation*} \mathcal{R}_d = \frac{\rho_d}{\rho_u} = \frac{\left( \gamma + 1\right) {\cal M}_u^2}{\gamma {\cal M}_u^2 +1- \left[({\cal M}_u^{2}-1)^2 -4 Q {\cal M}_u^{2} \right]^{1/2}}, \end{equation*}\] \[\begin{equation*} \mathcal{P}_d = \frac{p_d}{p_u} =\frac{\gamma {\cal M}_u^2 +1+ \gamma\left[({\cal M}_u^{2}-1)^2 -4 Q {\cal M}_u^{2} \right]^{1/2}}{\gamma + 1}, \label{eq:Pd} \end{equation*}\] for density and pressure, respectively. However, What would happen in case we have a non-homogeneous mixture? A better understanding of this can help in the development of rotating detonation engines –REFERENCES–. Fig. 1 is a sketch of the problem posed. The wrinkling of the detonation front can come from constructive or destructive interferences regarding the composition of the fuel (see Fig.2).

Objectives

  1. Obtain 2D and 3D integral formulas for numerical benchmarking.
  2. Understand from a theoretical perspective the effects on the propagation of the detonation.

Methods

  1. Formulate base-problem assuming fast-reaction model: Reactive Rankine-Hugoniot equations.
  2. Include mono-chromatic perturbations of the fuel mass fraction.
  3. Obtain weighted integrals considering an isotropic spectrum: Fourier analysis.
  4. Obtain corrections to Rankine–Hugoniot equations and speed propagation: Averaged turbulent conservation equations.
